\subsection{Test Suite}

The suite comprises 247 tests across 14 files. Module~1 tests validate compatibility scoring against known values (same key $\to 1.0$, relative major/minor $> 0.9$, tritone $< 0.5$). Module~2 tests verify beam search on synthetic graphs and mock all four ListenBrainz similarity calls. Module~3 tests cover all constraint classes with edge cases. Module~4 tests verify classifier convergence and pickle round-trips. Mocking external APIs keeps the full suite under three seconds.

\subsection{Performance}
\label{sec:results-perf}

Table~\ref{tab:benchmark} compares the Postgres mirror to the public MusicBrainz REST API.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Postgres mirror vs.\ public MusicBrainz REST API (mean of 3 runs).}
\label{tab:benchmark}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrr@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Operation} & \textbf{API (s)} & \textbf{Postgres (s)} & \textbf{Speedup} \\
\midrule
25 single lookups & 43.5 & 24.9 & 1.75$\times$ \\
25-MBID batch & 1.16 & 0.93 & 1.25$\times$ \\
5 artist-relationship queries & 6.3 & 2.3 & 2.74$\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Relationship traversal sees the largest gain (2.74$\times$) because each REST call only returns one neighbourhood, so multi-hop traversal becomes a chain of round-trips, whereas the Postgres mirror executes the same traversal as a single recursive query. Typical end-to-end playlist generation runs roughly 2$\times$ faster on the mirror.

\subsection{Constraint Satisfaction}

The min-conflicts solver achieves 100\% hard-constraint satisfaction on 50 test playlists (no repeated artists, no repeated tracks), reducing total soft-violation score by 67\% with a mean of 3.2 swap iterations to convergence.

\subsection{Mood Classification}

The mood classifier reached a maximum single-split validation accuracy of 64.7\% across the six classes, with most residual confusion between perceptually adjacent moods (chill / calm). \subsection{End-to-End Generation and Demo Experience}

The demoed flow has two steps. First, the user selects a source and destination track via a MusicBrainz canonical search field that resolves recording titles to MBIDs; then the user sets the desired playlist length and beam width before submitting. The request is posted to \texttt{/api/playlist} and the result page (Figure~\ref{fig:webinterface}) displays the full track list alongside a per-transition breakdown. In practice, playlist generation is severely throttled by the AcousticBrainz API: each candidate track requires a low-level and high-level feature fetch, and sustained requests trigger rate limiting that we were unable to overcome, pushing end-to-end generation time from the intended seconds into the range of minutes to hours.

Figure~\ref{fig:webinterface} shows a representative run: source \emph{Girls Just Want to Have Fun} (Cyndi Lauper) to destination \emph{Comfortably Numb} (Pink Floyd), \texttt{length=12}, \texttt{beam\_width=10}. Beam search returned an 11-track path (one short of the requested length) with a total cost of 2.919 and a mean transition compatibility of 71\%. Each of the 10 transitions is displayed with seven component scores (key, tempo, energy, loudness, mood, timbre, genre); compatibility ranged from 67\% to 77\%. One key-compatibility violation was flagged (D~major $\to$ C~minor at transition~6). The constraint scorecard shows no repeated tracks (100\%, pass), tempo smoothness (100\%, pass), and genre variety (80\%, fail---the path stays predominantly in classic rock). An auto-generated journey summary reads: ``A journey through pop $\to$ rock $\to$ rhythm \& blues $\to$ jazz \ldots, dipping then rising across 11 tracks.''
